Definition

What Is Alexinomia?

Alexinomia (from Greek: alex — "without" + noma — "name") is a psychological phenomenon in which a person knows someone's name perfectly well but experiences severe anxiety or discomfort when attempting to use it in conversation.

First formally identified and named by researchers at Sigmund Freud Private University Vienna, alexinomia is not a memory disorder, a language impairment, or a simple social quirk. It is a distinct psychosocial phenomenon driven by anxiety — one that can silently affect how people connect with others every single day.

The avoidance can occur with any name in any relationship: friends, partners, colleagues, even family members. Rather than saying "Hi, Sarah!" a person with alexinomia might say "Hey!" and change the subject — or send a text instead of calling, purely to avoid the moment of having to say the name out loud.

📖

Etymology

The word means "no words for names" — coined to capture the specific blockage experienced: the name is known, but cannot be spoken.

🧠

Not Memory Loss

Alexinomia is unrelated to forgetting names. People with alexinomia know the name — the barrier is emotional, not cognitive.

🌍

Cross-Cultural

Research indicates alexinomia occurs across many languages, cultures, and age groups — it is not unique to any one society or setting.

Signs & Experience

How Does It Manifest?

Alexinomia is not a checklist — it is a lived experience. Click each card to learn more.

😰

Anticipatory Anxiety

Click to expand

Before a conversation even begins, a person with alexinomia may feel dread about the moment they would typically address someone by name. This anticipation can cause avoidance of entire social situations.

🚫

Active Name Avoidance

Click to expand

Individuals go to considerable lengths to avoid saying names directly — rephrasing sentences, using "hey," "excuse me," or non-verbal gestures instead. The avoidance happens across text, phone, and face-to-face communication.

💔

Relationship Strain

Click to expand

Research shows the behavior is paradoxically stronger in closer relationships. Partners and family members may notice the absence of their name and misinterpret it as emotional distance, indifference, or a lack of love — when the reality is quite the opposite.

🧊

Physical Discomfort

Click to expand

Beyond emotional distress, many individuals report physical sensations — tension, a feeling of freezing up, an inability to form the word — in the moment they attempt to say a name. These somatic responses reinforce avoidance.

🙈

Shame & Secrecy

Click to expand

Because the phenomenon was previously unnamed and unstudied, many people experiencing it had no framework to understand it. This often led to deep shame and secrecy — the person knowing something feels "wrong" but not knowing why, or even that others share the experience.

🔗

Insecure Attachment

Click to expand

Research has found that those with alexinomia often show higher attachment insecurity — specifically discomfort with emotional closeness and intimacy. Saying someone's name is an act of intimacy, and for those with anxious or avoidant attachment styles, this intimacy can feel threatening.

Peer-Reviewed Science

The Research Story

Alexinomia is a recently identified phenomenon. Here is how scientific understanding has developed.

2023
Frontiers in Psychology

The Naming of a Phenomenon

In the first peer-reviewed paper on the topic, Thomas Ditye, Nina Rodax, and Lisa Welleschik at Sigmund Freud Private University Vienna formally named and defined alexinomia. Drawing on qualitative observation and preliminary data, the researchers proposed the term and outlined its key characteristics: anxiety-driven name avoidance that is distinct from memory problems and linked to insecure attachment and social anxiety.

The paper introduced the framework that would guide all subsequent research, establishing that alexinomia operates across all types of relationships and communication modalities.

Citation: Ditye, T., Rodax, N., & Welleschik, L. (2023). Alexinomia: The fear of using personal names. Frontiers in Psychology, 14, 1129272. doi:10.3389/fpsyg.2023.1129272
2024
Acta Psychologica

Confirming a Widespread Phenomenon

Bergert, Welleschik, and Ditye published a large-scale qualitative study analyzing 257 unique sources — personal accounts, forum posts, and testimonials — to examine alexinomia as a broad psychosocial phenomenon. The study confirmed that alexinomia is far from rare, appearing across diverse demographic and cultural groups.

The research characterized alexinomia as "an inhibited behaviour in saying names" that is psychosocially distinct, and noted the phenomenon had been widely experienced but remained unnamed and unacknowledged before the 2023 paper.

Citation: Bergert, A., Welleschik, L., & Ditye, T. (2024). "Why can't I say people's names?" Alexinomia as a widespread psychological phenomenon. Acta Psychologica, 246, 104279. PMID: 38643557
2025
Journal of Anxiety Disorders

Linking Alexinomia to Social Anxiety

Ditye, Sartorio, and Welleschik tested a clinical hypothesis in a controlled study of 190 participants: that alexinomia is not just correlated with social anxiety, but may represent an under-recognized symptom of it. Results showed a strong positive relationship between social anxiety severity and degree of name-avoidance behavior.

The study found that name avoidance was particularly pronounced in contexts of direct verbal communication and in closer personal relationships — precisely the situations where social anxiety is most activating. The authors concluded that alexinomia warrants formal consideration within the clinical diagnosis and treatment of social anxiety disorder.

Citation: Ditye, T., Sartorio, M., & Welleschik, L. (2025). Name avoidance in social anxiety: Understanding alexinomia. Journal of Anxiety Disorders. PMID: 39721365

What the Research Tells Us

  • Alexinomia is real and measurable — it is not simply shyness or forgetfulness.
  • It is linked to social anxiety — higher anxiety correlates with more severe name avoidance.
  • Insecure attachment plays a role — those who feel uncomfortable with emotional closeness are more affected.
  • It crosses cultures and languages — research suggests the phenomenon appears in multiple linguistic and sociocultural contexts.
  • It is more intense in close relationships — paradoxically, the people we care about most are often the hardest to name.
  • It impacts quality of life — avoidance behaviors can damage relationships and reduce social functioning.
Living With Alexinomia

The Real-World Impact

Alexinomia is not merely an awkward habit — it shapes how people experience their closest relationships.

Romantic Relationships

Partners may feel unnamed — and therefore unloved or unimportant. The absence of a name in intimate speech is often noticed even if the reason is never understood. Research found that those with alexinomia show elevated discomfort with emotional closeness, suggesting that the fear of naming is bound up with the fear of true intimacy.

Friendships

Friendships can feel one-sided or distant to the person without alexinomia, who may not understand why their name is never used. The person with alexinomia, meanwhile, may develop elaborate strategies — always being the one to text first, preferring group chats, or deflecting direct address — without ever knowing why these workarounds feel necessary.

The Workplace

Professional settings add their own pressure. Failing to address a colleague or manager by name can be perceived as disrespectful or cold. Networking events, introductions, and team meetings become landmines, and the effort required to navigate them can be exhausting and career-limiting.

Mental Health

The accumulation of avoided moments, misunderstood relationships, and unexplained shame carries a mental health cost. Because the phenomenon was previously unnamed, many people experienced their alexinomia in isolation — unable to explain it, and unaware that others shared it.

Why Names Feel So Intimate

Research suggests that using someone's name is a fundamentally intimate act — it signals recognition, attention, and personal connection. Dale Carnegie famously called a person's name "the sweetest sound" to them. For those with alexinomia, this very intimacy is the source of anxiety: saying a name feels like an exposure of closeness that feels unbearable, dangerous, or simply impossible.

This connects closely to findings on attachment theory: individuals with anxious or avoidant attachment styles — who are more likely to feel threatened by closeness — show higher rates of alexinomia symptoms. The name becomes a symbol of the intimacy they cannot yet allow themselves.

Support & Treatment

Getting Help

Research is still young, but existing knowledge of related conditions points toward effective paths forward.

🧩

Psychotherapy

Researchers recommend working with a trained psychotherapist or clinical psychologist. Cognitive Behavioural Therapy (CBT) — particularly approaches developed for social anxiety — is the most evidence-based starting point, as alexinomia is closely linked to social anxiety disorder.

🔗

Attachment-Informed Therapy

Because research links alexinomia to insecure attachment, therapies that address attachment patterns — including Emotionally Focused Therapy (EFT) and schema-based approaches — may be especially relevant for understanding the emotional roots of name avoidance.

💬

Naming It

For many people, simply learning that alexinomia has a name — and that it is not unusual — provides immediate relief. The researchers at Sigmund Freud Private University Vienna have noted that people who discovered the term reported feeling "seen" for the first time after years of unexplained shame.

📣

Communicate Openly

If you experience alexinomia and it affects your relationships, consider sharing what you have learned with those close to you. Understanding can transform misinterpretation ("they don't care about me") into empathy ("they are struggling with something real").
