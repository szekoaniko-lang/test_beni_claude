A quiet, under-researched anxiety shapes how millions of people speak — or fail to speak — the names of partners, parents, friends, and colleagues. This is an evidence-based guide to what clinicians now call alexinomia.

01 · Definition

What is alexinomia?

Alexinomia is a fear-based avoidance of using personal names in conversation. It is not forgetfulness; affected people know the name perfectly well. The block is emotional — a sudden wave of anxiety, awkwardness, or shame the moment they try to say it aloud.

The term was coined in 2023 by a research group at Sigmund Freud Private University (Vienna) after interviews revealed a pattern of severe, lifelong name-avoidance that had never been named in the clinical literature. The word joins the Greek a- (without) + lexi- (word) + -nomia (name).

In one sentence

An anxiety-driven inability to address others by name — across every form of communication and every kind of relationship.

  • Not a memory problem
  • Not shyness about speaking in general
  • Often worse with closer people, not strangers
  • Strongly tied to social anxiety and insecure attachment

02 · The inner experience

What it feels like, from the inside

Across 257 first-person accounts analysed by Bergert, Welleschik & Ditye (2024), a small set of experiences came up again and again. Tap any card to expand.

  • I.

    Anticipatory dread

    A spike of anxiety before the name is even spoken.

    Affected individuals describe rehearsing the name in their head, scripting the sentence, then freezing at the last moment. The dread is not about saying the wrong name — it is about the act of naming itself.

  • II.

    Elaborate avoidance

    Everyday workarounds invented to dodge the name.

    "Hey you," "excuse me," waving, texting instead of calling, waiting to make eye contact before speaking. Some people avoid partners' names for years — one participant reported never saying her husband's name in fifteen years of marriage.

  • III.

    Closer = harder

    Intimacy intensifies, rather than eases, the block.

    Counter-intuitively, alexinomia is typically most severe with partners, parents, and close friends. Strangers and acquaintances are sometimes easier — a pattern that points to attachment-related vulnerability rather than general social fear.

  • IV.

    Physical sensations

    A body that rebels at the first syllable.

    Tight throat, held breath, flushed face, racing heart, a sense of the name "sticking" in the mouth. Several accounts describe the feeling as physically catching the name before it leaves the lips.

  • V.

    Shame and secrecy

    A private struggle rarely spoken about.

    Because the behaviour looks trivial from the outside, most affected people have never told anyone. Many first recognise themselves only when they stumble on someone else's account — one reason alexinomia was invisible in the literature for so long.

  • VI.

    Relational cost

    Loved ones wonder why they are never named.

    Partners and parents sometimes interpret the avoidance as coldness or rejection. The 2023 interview study found this misreading to be one of the most painful downstream consequences of the condition.

03 · The evidence

Three papers, one emerging picture

Alexinomia is a young field — the term itself is only three years old. Here is the full peer-reviewed literature, in order.

  1. 2023

    Frontiers in Psychology · open access

    Alexinomia: The fear of using personal names

    Ditye, T., Rodax, N., & Welleschik, L.

    The paper that named the phenomenon. A mixed-methods study of 13 affected women combined semi-structured interviews with standardised measures of social anxiety and attachment. The authors found that name-avoidance was most severe in close relationships, correlated with social-anxiety and insecure-attachment scores, and operated across every communication channel — face-to-face, phone, text, and written address.

    Read the paper ↗
  2. 2024

    Acta Psychologica · Elsevier

    "Why can't I say people's names?" Alexinomia as a widespread psychological phenomenon

    Bergert, A., Welleschik, L., & Ditye, T.

    A qualitative analysis of 257 first-person accounts pulled from English-language advice forums and discussion threads. The study documents the phenomenon across demographics and geographies, catalogues effective and ineffective coping strategies, and shows that severity can diminish with deliberate practice — the first evidence that alexinomia is trainable rather than fixed.

    Read the paper ↗
  3. 2025

    Journal of Anxiety Disorders · Elsevier

    Name avoidance in social anxiety: Understanding alexinomia

    Ditye, T., Sartorio, M., & Welleschik, L.

    The first quantitative test in a larger sample (n = 190). Using the Social Interaction Anxiety Scale and the Liebowitz Social Anxiety Scale, the authors found a strong positive relationship between social-anxiety severity and fear-based name avoidance — positioning alexinomia as a likely under-recognised symptom of social-anxiety disorder.

04 · Where it lands

How alexinomia reshapes a life

Because names are such small words, the cost of avoiding them is paid quietly — across thousands of tiny interactions.

Romantic life

Partners who are never addressed by name sometimes read the silence as emotional withdrawal. Affected individuals describe feeling "stuck behind glass" — loving someone deeply while being unable to say the one word that most intimately acknowledges them.

Family of origin

Interview participants frequently reported that the block first appeared in childhood around a parent's name. For some, it never shifted; decades later the parent's name still cannot be spoken.

Friendships

Forming new friendships is complicated by a reluctance to use the friend's name — which can read as aloofness. Group dynamics are especially hard; addressing one person in a group makes the avoidance more visible.

Work and school

Professional settings demand frequent address by name — greetings, introductions, meetings, emails. Affected people report avoiding roles with high interpersonal-naming load, narrowing their career choices.

06 · Support

What helps — according to the research

There is no manualised treatment for alexinomia yet. But the condition's strong links to social anxiety and insecure attachment point to well-established therapeutic approaches.

CBT for social anxiety

Because alexinomia tracks so closely with social-anxiety severity (Ditye et al., 2025), the exposure-based techniques and cognitive restructuring used in CBT for social anxiety are a natural starting point — graduated practice, beginning with low-stakes names and working toward close relationships.

Attachment-informed therapy

The 2023 study's finding that alexinomia intensifies in close relationships, and correlates with insecure-attachment measures, suggests attachment-focused modalities (e.g. emotionally focused therapy) may reach parts of the problem that pure exposure does not.

Naming, literally

The single most-reported benefit in Bergert et al. (2024) was simply discovering that the experience had a name. Many affected people had carried the block in silence for decades; being able to say "I have alexinomia" is itself a first therapeutic step.
